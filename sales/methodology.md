# How to Sell - The Method

The tactical, repeatable method for turning a prospect into a well-fit, paying customer. It expands the
sales [workflows](workflows.md) and [SOPs](sops.md) into a how-to, and rests on
[buyer psychology](psychology.md) and the principle that you sell **fit and value, never hype**. Founders
should run this themselves for the first ~100 customers to learn what resonates before systematizing it.

## The method, end to end

```
Prospect -> Qualify -> Discover -> Demonstrate value -> Propose
         -> Handle objections -> Negotiate -> Close -> Hand off
```

## 1. Prospect

Identify accounts that fit the ideal customer profile ([prospecting](../agents/sales/prospecting-agent.md)).
Personalize outreach around a likely problem, not a generic pitch. Quality of targeting beats volume.

## 2. Qualify

Confirm this is a real opportunity before investing time. Two common frameworks:

- **BANT** - Budget, Authority, Need, Timing.
- **MEDDIC** - Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify pain, Champion.

Qualify *out* quickly and honestly - time spent on a bad-fit deal is time stolen from a good one.

## 3. Discover (the heart of selling)

Great selling is mostly listening. Understand the buyer's world before proposing anything:

- What is the problem, and what does it cost them today (time, money, risk)?
- What does success look like - specifically and measurably?
- What have they tried? Why hasn't it worked?
- Who decides, and how does a purchase actually get approved?

Ask about their **life and past behavior**, not hypotheticals (the [Mom Test](../strategy/idea-validation.md)).
Talk less than the buyer. The [discovery notes example](examples/discovery-notes-example.md) shows the output.

## 4. Demonstrate value

Show the solution **tailored to their stated problem**, not a feature tour. Connect each capability to a
pain they described. Quantify the outcome and the **cost of inaction** - the strongest motivator is often
the pain of staying the same ([psychology](psychology.md)).

## 5. Propose

A proposal anchored on **value and outcome**, not a feature list. Make the price a fraction of the value
delivered. Keep terms within [policy](../agents/sales/deal-desk-agent.md); route contracts through
[legal](../legal/README.md).

## 6. Handle objections

Objections are requests for information or unresolved fears ([psychology](psychology.md#the-psychology-of-objections)).
A simple loop:

1. **Acknowledge** - "That's a fair concern."
2. **Understand** - ask what's really behind it.
3. **Respond** - address the real risk with evidence.
4. **Confirm** - "Does that resolve it?"

Never argue; never dismiss. "Too expensive" usually means "not yet convinced of the value."

## 7. Negotiate

Anchor on value. **Trade, don't cave** - if you give something (discount), get something (longer term,
case study, faster decision). Protect price; discount deliberately and within policy, never reflexively.

## 8. Close (ethically)

Closing is asking for the decision once value and fit are established. Summarize the value, confirm fit,
and ask directly. Create **genuine** urgency only - a real deadline, a real cost of delay, a real capacity
limit ([the ethical line](psychology.md#the-ethical-line)). Offer a small next step (a pilot) when a full
commitment is premature. Never manufacture pressure.

## 9. Hand off

A closed deal is the start of the relationship. Complete a full handoff to
[customer-success](../customer-success/README.md) so the customer reaches value - because retention, not
the signature, is where the money is ([deal-handoff checklist](checklists/deal-handoff-checklist.md)).

## The throughline

Sell to people you can genuinely help, tell them the truth, anchor on their outcome, and make it easy to
say yes to a good decision. That is how you sell in a way that compounds into retention and referrals.
