# Buyer Psychology & Ethical Persuasion

People do not make purely rational buying decisions - they decide emotionally and justify logically.
Understanding *how* buyers actually decide lets you communicate genuine value clearly and remove the
friction between their problem and your solution. This is persuasion in service of a good decision.

> **Persuasion is not manipulation.** Persuasion helps someone make a decision that genuinely serves them,
> with honest information. Manipulation pushes someone toward a decision against their interest using
> deception or pressure. Company OS only ever does the former - see [the ethical line](#the-ethical-line)
> and the sales principle of [honesty](principles.md). Manipulation wins a deal and loses a customer.

## How buyers actually decide

- **Emotion first, logic second.** People feel the decision, then justify it with reasons. Speak to both.
- **The biggest competitor is "do nothing."** Status-quo bias is powerful; the cost of *inaction* is
  often your strongest argument.
- **Loss aversion.** People fear losing more than they value gaining. Framing around avoided pain and risk
  often lands harder than framing around upside - when both are true.
- **Trust precedes features.** No one buys from someone they do not trust, however good the product.
- **Cognitive load kills deals.** Confusion and too many choices lead to "no." Simplicity converts.

## The principles of influence (applied ethically)

The classic levers of influence, each with its honest application - and its abuse to avoid:

| Principle | Honest use | Abuse to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Reciprocity** | Give real value first (a useful trial, insight, help) | Manufactured "favors" that obligate |
| **Commitment & consistency** | Small, genuine yeses that build toward fit | Tricking someone into a commitment |
| **Social proof** | Show real customers like them succeeding | Fake reviews or invented logos |
| **Authority** | Demonstrate genuine expertise and credibility | Faking credentials or endorsements |
| **Liking** | Build real rapport; find true common ground | Insincere flattery |
| **Scarcity** | Communicate *real* limits (capacity, a real deadline) | Fake countdowns and false "last chance" |
| **Unity** | Shared identity and goals that genuinely exist | Manufactured tribalism |

## Other decision levers

- **Anchoring.** The first number sets the reference point; anchor on value delivered, not on cost.
- **Framing.** The same fact framed as a gain vs. a loss lands differently - use the true frame that
  matches the buyer's real stakes.
- **The endowment effect.** People value what they already feel is theirs - a hands-on trial creates
  genuine ownership (only valuable if the product actually delivers).
- **Decision simplicity.** Fewer, clearer options convert better than an overwhelming menu.
- **Peak-end rule.** People remember the emotional peak and the ending of an interaction - make both good.

## The psychology of objections

Objections are not rejection - they are **requests for information** or signals of an **unresolved risk or
fear**. Behind "it's too expensive" is often "I'm not convinced of the value" or "I fear this won't work."
Address the emotion and the underlying risk, not just the surface words (see
[objection handling](methodology.md#6-handle-objections)).

## The ethical line

Non-negotiable, and consistent with the sales [principles](principles.md) and
[standards](standards.md):

- **Never sell to a poor-fit buyer.** If the product will not help them, say so. Fit customers stay; misfits churn and warn others.
- **Never oversell.** Set expectations the product will meet or exceed.
- **Never fake scarcity, proof, or authority.** Deception detected once destroys trust permanently.
- **Never exploit vulnerability or pressure past a genuine "no."**

Ethical persuasion compounds into trust, retention, and referrals. Manipulation compounds into churn,
refunds, and reputation damage. Honesty is the long-term optimum. This mindset also powers
[marketing persuasion](../marketing/psychology.md).
