# Idea Validation

Between an [idea](../research/ideation.md) and building a [product](../product/README.md) sits the phase
that saves companies from building things nobody wants. The goal is not to prove yourself right - it is
to **try to prove yourself wrong quickly and cheaply**, before you spend months and money.

> **The single most expensive mistake a company can make is building something nobody wants.** Validation
> is the insurance policy, and it is cheap.

## Start with the riskiest assumption

Every idea rests on a stack of assumptions. List them, then rank by risk: *how likely is this to be
false, and how fatal if it is?* Test the riskiest one first, with the cheapest test that can move your
confidence. Common riskiest assumptions:

- **Problem:** does this problem exist, is it frequent, and is it painful enough to pay to solve?
- **Solution:** does our approach actually solve it?
- **Willingness to pay:** will people pay (with money, not compliments)?
- **Reachability:** can we reach these customers affordably? ([channels](../sales/channels.md))

## The validation ladder (cheapest to most expensive)

Climb only as far as each rung's evidence justifies. Stop the moment you are convinced it fails.

| Rung | Test | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Desk research | Market data, competitors, forums | The problem and market plausibly exist ([research](../research/README.md)) |
| 2. Problem interviews | Talk to 10-30 target users about their life | The problem is real, frequent, and painful ([discovery](../product/discovery.md)) |
| 3. Landing page / smoke test | A page describing the offer; measure sign-ups | People want the promised outcome |
| 4. Fake door | A "buy" button that leads to a waitlist | Intent to act, not just interest |
| 5. Concierge / Wizard of Oz | Deliver the outcome manually, no product | The solution actually helps; people value it |
| 6. Pre-sales / LOIs | Take money or signed letters of intent before building | Real willingness to pay |
| 7. Paid pilot | A small paying deployment | It works and retains in the real world |

Only after this does building a full [MVP](../../playbooks/building-an-mvp.md) make sense.

## Signals that count vs. signals that lie

**Trust:** people pay · they use it repeatedly · they refer others unprompted · they would be "very
disappointed" if it vanished (the Sean Ellis PMF test) · they are already hacking together a workaround.

**Distrust:** "That's a great idea" · "I would totally use that" · vanity sign-ups with no activation ·
enthusiasm from friends · your own excitement. Compliments are not evidence.

> **The Mom Test:** ask about their life and past behavior, not about your idea. "Walk me through the last
> time you dealt with X" beats "would you use a tool that does X?" People lie to be nice; behavior does not.

## Define kill criteria in advance

Before running a test, write down the result that would kill or pivot the idea. Deciding after you see the
data invites rationalization. Pre-commit: "If fewer than N of 20 interviewees describe this as a top-three
pain, we drop it." This protects against falling in love with a bad idea.

## Output

A validated (or invalidated) problem with real evidence, a confidence level, and a decision:
**pursue → [define the product](../product/requirements.md)**, **pivot**, or **drop**. Validated ideas
enter the [startup lifecycle](startup-lifecycle.md); the process is run via the
[validating-an-idea playbook](../../playbooks/validating-an-idea.md).
