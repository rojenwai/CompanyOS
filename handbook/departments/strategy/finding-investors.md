# Where to Find Investors

Raising capital is a sales process, and investors are the customers. This page covers **who** funds
companies at each stage, **where** to find them, and **how** to get in the door. It pairs with
[how to pitch](pitching.md) and the [fundraising playbook](../../playbooks/fundraising.md). First decide
whether to raise at all - see [raise vs. bootstrap](unit-economics.md).

## Match the source to your stage

| Stage | Typical funders | Typical check |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-seed** | Founders' savings, friends & family, angel investors, accelerators, pre-seed micro-VCs, grants, crowdfunding | $10k-$500k |
| **Seed** | Angels & angel groups, seed VCs, micro-VCs, accelerators | $500k-$3M |
| **Series A+** | Institutional VCs | $3M+ |
| **Any stage** | Revenue-based financing, venture debt, strategic/corporate VC, family offices, government grants (non-dilutive) | varies |

Raising from the wrong stage of investor wastes everyone's time - a Series A fund will not write a
pre-seed check, and an angel cannot lead your Series A.

## Where to actually find them

- **Warm introductions (by far the best).** A trusted mutual connection referring you converts far better
  than cold outreach. The intro *is* the first filter investors apply.
- **Portfolio-founder intros.** Founders an investor has already funded are the strongest referrers - and
  they will tell you honestly what that investor is like to work with.
- **Angel networks & syndicates.** AngelList syndicates, local angel groups, sector-specific angel networks.
- **Accelerators & incubators.** Y Combinator, Techstars, and regional/vertical programs - they provide
  capital, mentorship, and a demo day that puts you in front of many investors at once.
- **Investor databases.** Crunchbase, PitchBook, OpenVC, and similar - research who invests in your stage
  and sector, and their check size, before reaching out.
- **Founder communities & events.** Founder Slack/Discord communities, industry conferences, pitch nights,
  and demo days.
- **Public presence.** Many investors are active and reachable on LinkedIn and X; a strong, specific
  message referencing a genuine connection can work when warm intros are unavailable.
- **Alumni & operator networks.** University alumni funds, former-employer networks, operator angels.

## Non-dilutive and alternative sources

Not every company should sell equity. Consider **government/innovation grants**, **revenue-based
financing**, **venture debt**, **rewards or equity crowdfunding**, and **strategic partnerships** -
especially when the business has revenue or the capital need is specific and time-boxed
([finance](../finance/README.md)).

## How to get the intro

1. Find the mutual connection (LinkedIn, portfolio founders, your network).
2. Give them an easy forwardable blurb: one line on what you do, your traction, and the ask.
3. Earn it - a warm intro spends the referrer's credibility, so make it easy to say yes.

## Choose your investors (diligence them back)

Money is not equal. Evaluate an investor on **fit** (stage, sector, check size), **value-add** (network,
expertise, help beyond capital), **reputation** (ask their portfolio founders, including ones that
failed), and **terms**. You will work with them for years - diligence them as hard as they diligence you.

## Run it like a pipeline

Treat fundraising like [sales](../sales/methodology.md): build a pipeline of many investors, run
conversations in parallel to create genuine urgency, track every stage, and keep momentum. A concentrated,
time-boxed process beats a slow trickle. Commitments require [CEO/CFO approval](../../../ai/orchestration/approval-engine.md);
the [Fundraising Agent](../../../ai/agents/strategy/fundraising-agent.md) assembles the pack and
[investor-relations](../investor-relations/README.md) keeps them warm afterward.
