# Startup Lifecycle — Stages 1–6

## Stage 1 — Problem Discovery

Find problems worth dedicating years to. A good problem is frequently experienced · expensive ·
time-consuming · emotionally frustrating · growing · poorly solved · has a large customer base · hard
to copy once solved.

**Problem evaluation score (1–10):** pain · frequency · urgency · market size · competition ·
innovation opportunity · scalability · defensibility · revenue potential · founder advantage · timing ·
technical feasibility. Low-scoring problems are archived.

## Stage 2 — Customer Discovery

Identify the primary customer, economic buyer, technical buyer, end user, decision maker, influencers,
champions, and blockers. Build [personas](../product/user-personas.md) and document
[Jobs To Be Done](../product/discovery.md) (functional · emotional · social) and current alternatives.

## Stage 3 — Market Research

Size the market: **TAM** (total addressable) · **SAM** (serviceable available) · **SOM** (serviceable
obtainable). Research industry size, growth, geography, regulations, technology trends, adoption curves,
economic drivers, and macro risks. See [market-analysis.md](market-analysis.md).

## Stage 4 — Competitor Intelligence

Study direct, indirect, and emerging competitors, open-source alternatives, and manual workflows. Use
[SWOT · Porter's Five Forces · PESTLE · Blue Ocean](market-analysis.md).

## Stage 5 — Business Model

Choose and validate the [business model, revenue, and pricing](business-model.md).

## Stage 6 — Product Definition

Define vision · mission · target customer · unique value proposition · competitive advantage · core
features · differentiators · roadmap · success metrics. Scope the [MVP](../../../ai/agents/product/mvp-planning-agent.md)
— the smallest product that solves one critical problem exceptionally well — and prioritize with
[RICE/MoSCoW/Kano](../product/decision-frameworks.md). Product definition is then owned by
[product](../product/README.md).
